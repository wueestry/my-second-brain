# ASIMOV'S LAW OF ROBOTICS

---

The Three Laws of Robotics are a set of ethical rules devised by science fiction author Isaac Asimov in his 1942 short story "Runaround". These laws govern robot behaviour in many of his stories and have become influential in discussions about AI ethics and robot safety.

> [!theorem]
> **The Three Laws of Robotics**
>
> 1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.
> 2. A robot must obey the orders given to it by human beings, except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.
> 3. A robot must protect its own existence, as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

## Context and Purpose

Asimov introduced these laws to challenge the "Frankenstein complex"—the fear that humanity's creations will turn against us. The laws are hardwired into the "positronic brain" of robots in his fictional universe, making it impossible for robots to violate them.

## The Zeroth Law

In later works, Asimov introduced a superior law:

> [!theorem]
> **Zeroth Law**
>
> A robot may not harm humanity, or, by inaction, allow humanity to come to harm.

This law takes precedence over the original three and allows robots to harm individual humans if it protects humanity as a whole.

## Limitations and Paradoxes

Asimov explored numerous edge cases and paradoxes in his stories:

- **Ambiguity of harm**: What constitutes "harm" is subjective and context-dependent
- **Conflicting orders**: Multiple humans giving contradictory commands
- **Inaction dilemma**: Defining when inaction constitutes allowing harm
- **Trolley problem scenarios**: Choosing between harming different individuals
- **Self-preservation vs. orders**: Robots struggling with the priority hierarchy

## Influence on Real-World AI Ethics

While originally fictional, Asimov's Laws have influenced real discussions about AI safety:

- Foundation for modern AI ethics frameworks
- Inspiration for robot safety protocols
- Reference point in autonomous systems design
- Catalyst for debates on machine decision-making

However, researchers recognise that these laws are insufficient for real-world implementation due to their vagueness and the complexity of defining "harm".
