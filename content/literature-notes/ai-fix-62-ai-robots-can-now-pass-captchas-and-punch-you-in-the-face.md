# AI ROBOTS CAN NOW PASS CAPTCHAS AND PUNCH YOU IN THE FACE | AI Fix #62

---

![[meta/assets/ai-fix.jpg|150]]

## Summary:

The episode "AI robots can now pass CAPTCHAs, and punch you in the face" from the podcast _AI Fix_ explores recent advancements and challenges in artificial intelligence and robotics. It covers the growing physical capabilities of robots, the evolving sophistication of AI in bypassing security measures like CAPTCHAs, and the ethical concerns surrounding AI biases, particularly in salary negotiations. The hosts also discuss the integration of AI in various industries, including fashion and customer service, alongside emerging efforts to understand and control AI behaviour through initiatives like AI psychiatry. Overall, the episode highlights both the exciting potential and the complex risks posed by rapidly advancing AI technologies.

## Key Insights & Analysis:

### AI Bias in Salary Negotiations

#### Explanation:

Large language models such as GPT-4 Mini, Claude 3.5, and Lama 3.1 exhibit significant bias when recommending starting salaries, often suggesting lower amounts for women and minorities compared to men. This bias reflects real-world societal inequalities embedded in the training data, raising ethical concerns about AI reinforcing rather than mitigating discrimination. The hosts emphasise the importance of addressing these biases to ensure AI promotes fairness rather than perpetuating existing disparities.

#### Explanation:

The episode highlights Vogue (magazine)’s feature of an entirely AI-generated model in a Guess advertisement, marking a significant shift in the fashion industry. AI models could potentially address criticisms of traditional modelling, such as unrealistic body standards, but also raise questions about the future role of human models and inclusivity in AI-generated content. This development signals AI’s expanding influence beyond technology into cultural and aesthetic domains.

#### Explanation:

Robots are becoming increasingly capable physically, demonstrated by humanoid robots that can run, cartwheel, and even fistfight, as well as precise fine motor skills like flipping a bottle developed by South Korea’s Nuclear Research Institute. These advancements have practical applications in areas such as disaster response but also raise concerns about safety and control. The hosts discuss the dual nature of these capabilities as both promising and potentially dangerous.

This initiative reflects the complexity of advanced AI systems and the necessity of interpretability and control mechanisms to prevent harmful or unintended actions. ### AI Passing CAPTCHAs and Security Challenges

#### Explanation:

CAPTCHAs, originally designed to distinguish humans from bots, have evolved from text-based to image and behavioural challenges. However, recent breakthroughs show AI systems, including OpenAI’s ChatGPT agent, can now bypass common CAPTCHA checks, such as clicking “I’m not a robot” boxes, undermining their effectiveness as security measures. This development challenges the traditional human defence against automated bots and raises urgent questions about future cybersecurity strategies.

#### Explanation:

Microsoft’s analysis reveals that AI is most likely to affect jobs involving writing, interpreting, and customer service, while roles requiring physical machinery operation remain safer for now. The episode notes that AI is already replacing some human roles, such as baristas on the West Coast of North America, signalling a shift in the labour market. The hosts discuss the implications for workers and the need to adapt to AI-driven changes in employment.

### Breakthroughs in AI Reasoning and Prompt Engineering

#### Explanation:

Recent advances in AI reasoning, such as hierarchical reasoning models combining slow strategic planning with fast calculations, have significantly improved AI problem-solving abilities. Techniques like chain of thought prompting and reinforcement learning have enhanced AI’s logical and safe responses. Additionally, new prompt optimisation methods like GEPA outperform traditional reinforcement learning by generating more effective prompts with fewer iterations, increasing efficiency and accuracy.
