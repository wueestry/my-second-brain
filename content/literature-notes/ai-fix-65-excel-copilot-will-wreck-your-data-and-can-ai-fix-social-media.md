# EXCEL COPILOT WILL WRECK YOUR DATA, AND CAN AI FIX SOCIAL MEDIA? | AI FIX #65

---

![[meta/assets/ai-fix.jpg|150]]

## Summary:

This episode of the podcast "News" explores two major themes: the introduction of Microsoft's AI-powered Excel Copilot and its potential risks, and a research experiment where AIs were given their own social media environment to study social dynamics and algorithmic effects. The hosts critically examine Microsoft's cautionary stance on relying on AI for accuracy in important tasks, highlighting concerns about de-skilling and trust in AI-generated data. They also discuss a Dutch study simulating social media with AI agents, revealing that algorithm tweaks have limited impact on echo chambers and polarisation, suggesting that user behaviour is the core issue. Throughout, the episode reflects on the broader implications of AI integration in everyday tools and social platforms, emphasising the need for careful governance and human oversight.

## Key Insights & Analysis:

### Microsoft's Excel Copilot: Promise and Peril

#### Explanation:

Microsoft has introduced an AI Copilot feature in Excel designed to simplify spreadsheet tasks through natural language commands. However, the company explicitly warns users not to rely on Copilot for tasks requiring accuracy, reproducibility, or legal and compliance implications, due to risks of errors and misinformation. This highlights a tension between AI’s convenience and its current limitations, raising concerns about over-reliance and the erosion of fundamental skills like formula use and data verification.

It also reflects on the psychological tendency to over-trust AI outputs without sufficient scrutiny.

### AI-Generated Social Media Experiment

#### Explanation:

Researchers in the Netherlands created a minimal social media platform populated entirely by AI agents with diverse personas to simulate human social dynamics. The experiment revealed that AI agents formed echo chambers, concentrated attention on popular users, and amplified polarising voices, mirroring real-world social media behaviour. Attempts to tweak algorithms (e.g., chronological feeds, hiding follower counts) had marginal effects, suggesting that user behaviour, not just algorithms, drives social media problems.

#### Timestamp References:

This insight highlights the complexity of social media issues and challenges the simplistic notion that algorithmic changes alone can fix polarisation and misinformation.

### The Limits of Algorithmic Interventions

#### Explanation:

The study tested six different algorithmic modifications to reduce polarisation and improve discourse but found that improvements in one area often came at the expense of another. For example, chronological feeds spread attention more evenly but amplified extreme voices. This demonstrates the inherent trade-offs in social media design and the difficulty of engineering perfect solutions.

### AI’s Role in De-skilling and Human Oversight

#### Explanation:

The episode discusses concerns that reliance on AI tools like Excel Copilot may contribute to the de-skilling of users, as people become less engaged with fundamental tasks such as formula creation and data validation. The hosts stress the importance of maintaining human skills and vigilance to detect AI errors, warning against complacency that could lead to significant mistakes or legal consequences.
